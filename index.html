How to Use This Playbook

Welcome to Winning in Your Marriage — a six-week cohort experience designed to give you and your spouse practical, biblical tools to strengthen your relationship. Whether your marriage feels rock solid or like it needs serious attention, this material will meet you where you are.

Each week includes four parts:

  • Scripture — a key passage that anchors the week's theme.
  • Teaching Notes — complete session notes with key concepts highlighted. Use these during your group session or as a personal review guide.
  • Couple Discussion — questions to answer privately with your spouse after the session. These are for you and your spouse only — you will not be asked to share with the group.
  • Weekly Challenge — one specific thing to do together before the next session.

A few ground rules for the cohort:

  • Don't use anything you learn as a weapon against your spouse.
  • Don't think primarily about what your spouse needs to learn — focus on yourself.
  • Don't judge or compare your marriage to others in the group.
  • Do take notes. Do the assignments. Do ask questions. And have fun.

Week Two

Love Her / Respect Him

Understanding what your spouse needs most
Scripture
"Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ... Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her... each one of you also must love his wife as he loves himself, and the wife must respect her husband." — Ephesians 5:21, 25, 33 (NIV)
Session Overview

Most marriage conflict can be traced back to one broken cycle: a wife who feels unloved, who then becomes disrespectful — and a husband who feels disrespected, who then becomes unloving. Dr. Emerson Eggerichs calls this the Crazy Cycle. Her primary need is unconditional love. His primary need is unconditional respect. This week we explore what that looks like in practice.

  • Complete this sentence together: "I feel most loved when you..." — then compare your answers.
  • Review the His Needs / Her Needs lists. Circle the top 3 needs on your list that your partner may not fully understand yet.
  • Where do you see the strongest overlap between what you need and what your partner instinctively gives?

Four Needs of Every Wife — RIBS

Every wife has four core needs that, when consistently met by her husband, create a foundation of love and security in the marriage.

R
Relationship
Women are relational by nature. Her deepest need is to be in an unconditional loving relationship — to be loved in spite of her flaws, not because of her performance.
I
Influence Authority
She needs to know she is her husband's number-one priority. Her words, thoughts, and feelings must carry weight with him. Cherish her and she will honor you. (Proverbs 4:8)
B
Beauty
Every wife needs to hear "You are beautiful." Not just on special occasions — regularly, sincerely, and specifically. If you don't tell her, someone else will.
S
Security
She needs a peaceful home free of worry — physical, financial, relational, and spiritual security. Her husband should be the one standing between her and whatever threatens the family.

Adapted from Love & Respect by Dr. Emerson Eggerichs.

Three Needs of Every Husband — RAS

Every husband has three core needs that, when consistently met by his wife, unlock his best self as a leader, lover, and protector.

R
Respect
God commands it in Ephesians 5:33. Respect is not contingent on how the husband behaves — it is given because of the position God has entrusted to him. Love is like food; respect is water. A man would rather live in a home without warmth than without respect.
A
Admiration
Where respect is about his position, admiration is about what he does. A wife's sincere admiration — for small things as much as big ones — drives a husband to become a better man, husband, and father.
S
Sex
For most husbands, physical intimacy is not primarily emotional — it's a core need. A healthy, consistent sex life is essential to a thriving marriage. Withholding sex is destructive. "Husbands and wives belong to each other." (1 Corinthians 7:4) We explored this in depth in Week 1.
Couple Discussion — Week 2

  2. Looking at RAS, which of the three needs do you feel your spouse provides well? Which one would you most like to see grow?
  3. In what area do you personally want to improve in providing for your spouse's needs? Be specific.

Healthy Intimacy

Building a vibrant, connected sex life

Session Overview

The enemy works on both ends of the same strategy: tempt you into sex before marriage, then after marriage do everything possible to keep you away from sex. A healthy sex life doesn't happen by accident — it requires intentionality, honesty, and mutual care. Below are nine practical tips for keeping your sex life strong.

Nine Tips for a Healthy Sex Life

1
Protect your sex life.
Build guardrails and boundaries. Be intentional about what you watch, hear, and read. Don't allow emotional connections with people of the opposite sex to form and deepen.
2
Stay physically fit.
How you care for your body is a form of care for your spouse. Physical health directly affects energy, confidence, and connection in the bedroom.
3
Never use sex as a weapon.
Withholding sex as punishment or leverage is destructive to trust and intimacy. Physical intimacy is a gift you give each other — not a bargaining chip.
4
Talk about it.
Have honest conversations about your desires, needs, and boundaries — outside the moment, not in the heat of it. Choose words carefully. This is a highly intimate topic that deserves private, respectful discussion between the two of you.
5
Keep your sex life a priority.
Life gets busy. Kids, work, and exhaustion are real — but so is the need for consistent physical connection. Frequency that works for you both is what matters. Build it into your life intentionally, not as an afterthought.
6
Fulfill one another's needs.
Shift your focus from your own cravings to meeting your spouse's needs. A marriage where both partners prioritize the other will never run dry.
7
Realize men and women differ.
Men tend to be visually stimulated and progress quickly. Women tend to be more relationally stimulated and need more time. Understanding these differences creates patience and grace for each other.
8
Take time for discovery.
You and your spouse's needs and preferences will change over time. Make it a regular, ongoing practice to learn what the other enjoys and desires.
9
View the whole day as foreplay.
Take the entire day to prepare the mental, emotional, and spiritual needs of each other. Talking, touching, and flirting over the course of the day heightens anticipation and makes the sacred time together something you both look forward to.

Week Three

Communicating Effectively

Learning to say it and hear it well
Scripture
"My dear brothers and sisters, take note of this: Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry." — James 1:19 (NIV)
Session Overview

Communication failure happens when one person feels misunderstood. For the most part, communication breaks down when people fail to express what they feel in a clear, non-threatening way — or fail to truly listen. Effective communication has two equally important parts.

SYMBIS Report
Communication — No new pages this week
Bring the Communication pages you received in Week 1.

Use your existing SYMBIS Communication section alongside this week's teaching. Specifically:

  • Revisit the "communication skills I'd like to improve" item you committed to in Week 1. How is it going?
  • Look at your talk style descriptions side by side. Where do your natural communication styles complement each other — and where do they create friction?
  • Which of the "tips for communicating with me" items from your report can you put into practice during this week's couple challenge?

Two Parts of Communicating Effectively

Part 1: Assertive Communication
Definition: The ability to express your feelings and ask for what you want in the relationship without being passive or aggressive.

No one can read your mind. If you want your spouse to understand you, you must share your thoughts and feelings in an effective manner so they can be received — not defended against.

Use "I" statements: "I feel..." and "I need..." rather than "You always..." or "You never..."
Part 2: Active Listening
Definition: Making a conscious effort to hear not only the words your spouse is saying, but more importantly, to understand the complete message being sent.

You then let your partner know you understand them by restating their message back to them — before you respond.

The goal is to listen to understand, not to listen to respond.

Five Guidelines for Active Listening

1.
Pay attention
Eliminate distractions. Put down your phone. Make eye contact. Give your full focus to your spouse — not just your ears.
2.
Show that you're listening
Use your body language. Nod. Lean in. Face your spouse. Your posture communicates whether you're genuinely present or just tolerating the conversation.
3.
Provide feedback
Tell your partner what you are hearing them say. "What I'm hearing you say is..." This confirms understanding and shows you were paying attention.
4.
Defer judgment
Do not get defensive or interrupt with counter-arguments while your spouse is still speaking. Let them finish. Your rebuttal can wait. First — understand.
5.
Respond appropriately
Do not get upset, angry, or display negative emotion when your spouse shares something difficult. Your emotional reaction determines whether they will trust you with honest communication again.
Couple Discussion — Week 3

This week's discussion is about how you communicate — not about the content of any particular disagreement. Keep it forward-focused.

  1. Of the five guidelines for active listening, which one is hardest for you personally? Why?
  2. When you're trying to communicate something important, what does your spouse do that makes you feel most heard?
  3. What is one change you could make this week to become a better listener for your spouse?
Weekly Challenge

This week, have one intentional conversation where you both practice assertive communication and active listening. Choose a topic that matters — something real — not just small talk. Before responding, each person must first summarize what they heard their spouse say. Only then may they respond.

Week Four

Fighting Fair

Turning conflict into connection
Scripture
"If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone." — Romans 12:18 (NIV)
Session Overview

Conflict is inevitable in marriage. But when couples learn to resolve conflict well, it becomes an opportunity to grow closer — not further apart. The problem is not that couples argue. Complaining and airing grievances are actually healthy marital activities. The problem is when conflict escalates into one of four toxic patterns.

SYMBIS Report

Use your SYMBIS Conflict pages alongside this week's teaching:

  • Look at your conflict style description. Does it feel accurate? What would your spouse add or correct?
  • Review your Hot Topics list together — the issues most likely to spark tension in your relationship. Identify the top 2–3 that have already surfaced between you.
  • Look at the Conflict Limiters for each of you. Which ones do you currently use? Which ones do you want to start using?
  • After completing your Peace Plan this week, return to the Hot Topics list and choose one topic to apply it to first.

Four Toxic Ways of Interacting

In order of least to most destructive:

Most Common
Criticism
Attacking your spouse's character rather than a specific behavior. Criticisms start with "You always..." or "You never..." Complaints address a behavior; criticism attacks the person.
More Damaging
Contempt
The intent to insult and psychologically abuse. Eye-rolling, mockery, name-calling, dismissiveness. Contempt communicates, "I am better than you." It is the single greatest predictor of divorce.
Even More Damaging
Defensiveness
Once contempt appears, defensiveness follows. Rather than taking any responsibility, the person being attacked defends themselves and counter-attacks. The original issue gets buried.
Most Destructive
Stonewalling
Shutting down completely — no eye contact, no response, no engagement. Emotionally overwhelming, predominantly experienced by men (85%). The conversation cannot continue or heal when one person has shut the door.

Nine Rules for Fighting Fair

1.
Choose your battles carefully.
90% of what couples fight about can be overlooked. Before raising a complaint, ask: is this worth it? Small things, over time, become the big things — so choose wisely, and let more go than you bring up.
2.
Practice assertiveness and active listening.
Apply what you learned in Week 3. Conflict is where these skills matter most. Are you actually hearing your spouse — or just waiting for your turn to respond?
3.
Discuss issues at the appropriate time.
You cannot have a rational conversation in a climate of hostility. Don't try to resolve things in the heat of the moment, or when either of you is tired, hungry, or stressed.
4.
No blaming.
Blaming only invites defensiveness and escalates the argument. It will not solve the problem. Focus your energy on finding a solution together, not assigning fault.
5.
Stay in the present.
Resist the urge to bring up past grievances. You can't change the past. Stick to one issue at a time. If you keep bringing up old wounds, it likely means those issues were never truly resolved — and that's a separate conversation.
6.
No yelling.
Yelling escalates everything and resolves nothing. If your emotions are running that high, step away and cool down. The meaning of your communication lies in how it lands — if it feels like yelling to your spouse, it is yelling.
7.
No degrading language.
Name-calling, insults, put-downs, and swearing show contempt for your spouse's dignity. Words cannot be unheard. Once spoken, they require significant work to heal.
8.
No use of force.
No physical force or threats of force — ever. This includes pushing, shoving, hitting, throwing things, or punching walls. Each person in this marriage has the right to feel completely safe. There are no exceptions to this rule.
9.
No talk of divorce.
Threatening to leave the relationship is manipulative and corrosive. It creates anxiety about abandonment and erodes the foundation of trust. Remove this word from your conflict vocabulary entirely.

Week Five

Money & Marriage

Building financial unity as a couple
Scripture
"Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Will he not first sit down and estimate the cost to see if he has enough money to complete it?" — Luke 14:28 (NIV)
Session Overview

Money is one of the leading causes of conflict and divorce. For men, finances are often tied to significance — the sense of being a provider and protector. For women, finances are often tied to security — the peace of mind that comes from knowing the family is stable. Understanding this difference is the first step toward financial unity.

SYMBIS Report
Finances & Roles — Distributed end of session
Two sections from your personal report will be handed out at the close of this session — take them home and work through them together.

Use your SYMBIS Finances and Roles pages to go deeper on what was taught today:

  • Review your spender/saver profiles side by side. Where do they align — and where do they pull in opposite directions?
  • Complete these sentence starters out loud together: "In my home growing up, money was..." and "When I think about our financial future..."
  • What money habits from your family of origin do you want to keep? Which ones do you want to leave behind?
  • From the Roles section: go through the household responsibilities list. Mark each item as His, Hers, or Shared. Where do your assumptions differ — and where is one of you assuming the other will handle it?

Ten Principles for Money & Marriage

1
Merge your finances.
Two becoming one is not just a spiritual principle — it's a financial one. "His money" and "her money" creates division. Build your financial life together.
2
Put God first in your finances.
Everything you have belongs to God. Giving is not an afterthought — it's the starting point. Couples who give regularly report less financial conflict and greater satisfaction in marriage.
3
Set goals together.
Something powerful happens when couples dream together. Where do you want to be in 5 years? 10? 20? Write it down. Goals you create together are goals you will actually pursue together.
4
Create a spending plan as a couple.
A budget is not a cage — it's a plan for your money that reflects your values and goals. Know how much you earn, where money is being spent, what you owe, and what you own.
5
Create an emergency fund for 3–6 months of expenses.
An emergency fund is the single most effective buffer against financial stress in marriage. When the car breaks down or a job is lost, you're ready — not reactive.
6
Minimize use of debts and credit cards.
Debt is borrowed future income. Every debt payment is money that can't go toward your goals. Work aggressively to become and remain debt-free.
7
Save a portion of every check.
Make saving automatic whenever possible. Set a goal to save at least 10% for the future. Pay yourself first, before discretionary spending begins.
8
Make monthly car and home expenses less than 7% and 20% of income, respectively.
These are practical guardrails to prevent housing and transportation from crowding out savings, giving, and everything else. If your numbers are higher, create a plan to bring them down over time.
9
Who pays the bills? Whoever is best at it.
This is not a male or female role. Determine which of you is more detail-oriented and consistent — and make that person the "CFO" of the household. The other spouse stays informed and involved, even if they're not the one paying.
10
Communicate about your finances.
Have a regular "money date" — a scheduled, low-pressure time to review your budget together, celebrate wins, and talk about adjustments. Transparency in finances creates trust.

Harmony & Expectations

Choosing real love — every day
Scripture
"Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud... It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. Love never fails." — 1 Corinthians 13:4, 7–8 (NIV)
Session Overview

Every marriage begins with the "in love" experience — that intoxicating, euphoric rush that makes you feel like you've found the one perfect person on earth. Studies show this feeling has an average lifespan of two years. Eventually, the fog lifts, the warts show up, and you are left with a choice. This week is about what you choose after that moment.

The "In Love" Experience vs. Real Love

The "In Love" Experience
  • Euphoric emotional obsession
  • Sees only the best in your partner
  • Effortless — driven by chemistry
  • Average lifespan: 2 years
  • Not a choice — it simply happens
  • Cannot sustain a marriage alone
Real Love
  • A daily choice and commitment
  • Sees flaws clearly and loves anyway
  • Requires effort, discipline, and sacrifice
  • Lifespan: a lifetime
  • A conscious decision to put the other first
  • The only kind that builds a great marriage

When the "in love" experience ends, there are three options:

  1. Resolve to live a life of misery with your spouse.
  2. Jump ship and try again (50% of marriages end in divorce).
  3. Recognize the "in love" experience for what it was — a temporary emotional high — and choose to pursue real love with your spouse.

Option three is what this cohort has been preparing you for. Real love begins with a declaration: "I am married to you, and I choose to look out for your interests."

The Five Love Languages

Each of us receives love in a different way. If you've been expressing love in your language and your spouse needs it in theirs, your efforts may not be landing. Understanding and speaking your spouse's primary love language is one of the most practical ways to sustain real love over a lifetime.

Love LanguageWhat It Looks LikeKey Need
Words of Affirmation Verbal compliments, words of appreciation and encouragement, kind words To feel appreciated and valued through words
Quality Time Undivided attention, face-to-face conversation, doing things together To feel chosen and prioritized
Receiving Gifts Thoughtful gifts (not necessarily expensive) that say "I was thinking of you" To feel remembered and cherished
Acts of Service Doing helpful things — cooking a meal, handling a task, easing a burden Actions speak louder than words
Physical Touch Hugs, hand-holding, a hand on the shoulder, sitting close Physical presence and connection

Appendix A: Our Peace Plan

"If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone." — Romans 12:18 (NIV)

Your Peace Plan is a written covenant between you and your spouse — an agreement about how you will handle conflict before it happens. Couples who have a plan go into conflict with tools, not just emotions. Review the example below, then write your own.

Example — John & Jane Doe's Peace Plan

Agreed upon: ___________________

We agree that we will attempt to keep our marriage as conflict-free as possible. When conflict occurs, this is our plan to deal with it constructively:

  • We will regularly schedule meetings to discuss relationship-related issues.
  • We declare date nights and special times together conflict-free — we agree not to discuss potentially divisive issues during these reserved times.
  • We commit to addressing conflict, or agreeing on a time to address it, before going to sleep at night.
  • We agree that if conflict is raised, the other spouse may defer the discussion to an agreed-upon later time.
  • We agree that when conflict is escalating, either of us can call a time-out.
  • We will avoid name-calling, abusive language, and use of force.
  • We vow never to use the word "divorce" in a heated conversation.
  • We will use assertive communication and active listening when we are struggling to communicate.
  • We commit to praying before and after dealing with conflict.
